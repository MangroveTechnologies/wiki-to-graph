# Language Models are Few-Shot Learners (GPT-3)

- **Authors:** Tom B. Brown, Benjamin Mann, Nick Ryder, Melanie Subbiah, Jared Kaplan, ... Ilya Sutskever, Dario Amodei (OpenAI)
- **Year:** 2020 (NeurIPS 2020)
- **arXiv:** 2005.14165 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.14165
- **PDF:** https://arxiv.org/pdf/2005.14165

## Abstract
Recent work has demonstrated substantial gains on many NLP tasks and benchmarks by pre-training on a large corpus of text followed by fine-tuning on a specific task. While typically task-agnostic in architecture, this method still requires task-specific fine-tuning datasets of thousands or tens of thousands of examples. By contrast, humans can generally perform a new language task from only a few examples or from simple instructions. Here we show that scaling up language models greatly improves task-agnostic, few-shot performance, sometimes even reaching competitiveness with prior state-of-the-art fine-tuning approaches. Specifically, we train GPT-3, an autoregressive language model with 175 billion parameters, 10x more than any previous non-sparse language model, and test its performance in the few-shot setting. For all tasks, GPT-3 is applied without any gradient updates or fine-tuning, with tasks and few-shot demonstrations specified purely via text interaction with the model. GPT-3 achieves strong performance on many NLP datasets. At the same time, we identify datasets where GPT-3's few-shot learning still struggles. Finally, we find that GPT-3 can generate news articles which human evaluators have difficulty distinguishing from human-written articles, and discuss broader societal impacts.

## Key points
- **175 billion parameters**, a decoder-only autoregressive Transformer; 10x larger than any previous dense LM.
- Introduces **in-context learning**: zero-shot, one-shot, and few-shot task specification purely via the prompt, with **no gradient updates / no fine-tuning**.
- Demonstrates **emergent capabilities** appearing with scale: on-the-fly reasoning, arithmetic, word unscrambling, using novel words.
- Reframes the pretrain→fine-tune paradigm (from BERT) toward pretrain→prompt.
- Limitations noted: struggles on some tasks, potential contamination from training on large web corpora, bias and societal-impact concerns.
